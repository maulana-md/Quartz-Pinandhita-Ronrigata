# Cleaning & Redrawing, or Editing

This part of scanlating entails cleaning up pages from texts, preparing it for [[typeset|typesetting]] later.

If you're working solo, the *cleaning* part is very much needed; for *redrawing*, you'll need to decide by yourself how clean you want your pages to be. In group setting, some group might have dedicated roles for each parts, and in some group both roles might be delegated into one. Check with your project manager or other members on the standard of page cleanliness they have; just ask whether some texts, like sound effects or environment texts, needs to be translated/typeset or not.

Your tools doesn't matter as much as your ability to clean and redraw. This group used Krita to do editing and typesetting; a lot of scanlation guides uses photoshop, especially older ones since it's easier to crack, or GIMP as free alternative. This page mostly contains general direction and advice on how to do editing work, and you can easily adapt those to fit the tool you have. Heck, I do remember CoolOtamegane, who edited good chunk of Onimai translation, started with Paint to edit (and Powerpoint to typeset; what a madman. They switched to CSP along the way, but still).

## Cleaning

This is the easy part: you just need to block out or "delete" texts on the pages with the surrounding background color. Simple color is enough! The default color would be white, though sometimes it might be black, like for black-colored text bubbles or boxes. For text with noticable border overlay, like sound effects or texts outside bubbles and boxes, its best to follow the border color; just make sure you only remove the text and block as few background elements as possible.

## Redrawing

For redrawing, there are several kind of redraw you'll encounter, from my experience with my own words: 1) black "elements", 2) plain color and gradient, and 3) textures. The goal is to make as if the removed text is never there in the first place.

"Do I need drawing skill to clean?"

The answer is no, but yes to be sure. No, because 80% of the work can be done with just clone tool or just simple brush, but every now and then you'll be faced with incomplete image that can only be fixed by drawing up the incomplete spaces. The more important thing, I think, is having the "sense" of how the original image should be.

### Black Image Elements

This is an umbrella term for lines and shapes in drawings, and for me, the easier kind to redraw since you mostly need the sense of how the lines and shapes should be on the bigger context.

The one that many people skips, but for our scanlation group is important, is the bubble shape.

Some texts might have been laid out superimposing it's bubble. After cleaning, you can see clearly that the bubble is disrupted by the text's overlay. Rather than following that text border, connect the two end point of the bubble following its context; smooth ones usually a little curved, and jagged, shouting one will have more pronounced curve and shape. Just make sure it fits how the bubble is shaped.

My general advice is don't be afraid to sketch first, especially if there's a lot to redraw! Your redraw doesn't need to be perfect or 100% similar to the artist's art style, it just need to fill what's there so that it doesn't feel jarring or incomplete.
